# Hangul Alphabet Report

## Overview
Hangul (한글) is the native alphabet of the Korean language, created in the 15 century by King **Sejong the Great** and scholars of the Joseon Dynasty. It is a **featural** writing system: the shapes of the letters (jamo, 자모) encode phonetic features such as place and manner of articulation. This report covers the basic jamo, the synthetic (compound) jamo, and the underlying linguistic theory that guides Hangul’s design.

---
### Core Principles (Hunminjeongeum Theory)
1. **Shape reflects articulation** –
   - **Consonants** are derived from the shape of the vocal tract when the sound is produced:
     - ⊥ (ㄱ) – the back of the tongue touching the soft palate (velar “k”).
     - ⊞ (ㄴ) – the tongue tip touching the alveolar ridge (alveolar “n”).
     - ○ (ㅁ) – closed lips for the bilabial “m”.
   - **Vowels** are constructed from three symbolic strokes that represent cosmic elements:
     - **·** (a dot) – the Sun (☀) or a round heaven.
     - **|** (a vertical line) – the human, who stands upright.
     - **─** (a horizontal line) – the earth.
     - Combining these yields the eight basic vowel shapes.
2. **Phonemic completeness** – The original set of 28 letters (14 consonants + 10 vowels) could represent every sound in Korean at the time.
3. **Ease of learning** – Because each glyph visually suggests its pronunciation, a learner can grasp the entire system in a few hours.

## Synthetic (Compound) Jamo
Hangul allows **combinations** of basic jamo to express additional consonant tensions and vowel qualities.

### Complex Vowels (Dipthongs & Triphthongs)
Complex vowels are created by stacking or juxtaposing the three elemental strokes.
- **Horizontal‑vertical combos** (ㅗ + ㅏ → ㅘ, ㅗ + ㅐ → ㅙ, ㅗ + ㅣ → ㅚ)
- **Vertical‑horizontal combos** (ㅜ + ㅓ → ㅝ, ㅜ + ㅔ → ㅞ, ㅜ + ㅣ → ㅟ)
- **Tri‑stroke** (ㅢ = ㅡ + ㅣ)
These are written as a single glyph but encode two or three vowel qualities.

### Final (받침) Consonant Clusters
Korean syllables can end with one or more consonants, written below the primary jamo block. The most common clusters are:
- **Simple finals**: ㄱ, ㄴ, ㄷ, ㄹ, ㅁ, ㅂ, ㅅ, ㅇ, ㅈ, ㅊ, ㅋ, ㅌ, ㅍ, ㅎ
- **Double (complex) finals**: ㄳ (ㄱ+ㅅ), ㄵ (ㄴ+ㅈ), ㄶ (ㄴ+ㅎ), ㄺ (ㄹ+ㄱ), ㄻ (ㄹ+ㅁ), ㄼ (ㄹ+ㅂ), ㄽ (ㄹ+ㅅ), ㄾ (ㄹ+ㅌ), ㄿ (ㄹ+ㅍ), ㅀ (ㄹ+ㅎ), ㅄ (ㅂ+ㅅ)
These clusters affect the phonetic realization, often simplifying to a single sound (e.g., ㄳ → /k/).

## Syllable Block Construction
A Hangul **syllable block** (글자) consists of:
1. **Initial consonant** (초성) – mandatory; if the sound begins with a vowel, the placeholder ㅇ is used.
2. **Medial vowel** (중성) – placed to the right of or below the initial, depending on its shape.
3. **Optional final consonant** (종성) – placed beneath the initial‑vowel pair.

### Geometric Layout Rules
- **Vertical vowels** (ㅏ, ㅓ, ㅣ) → final placed directly underneath.
- **Horizontal vowels** (ㅗ, ㅜ, ㅡ) → final placed to the right of the vowel.
- **Complex vowels** (ㅘ, ㅝ, ㅢ, …) → final placed beneath the combined shape.

### Examples
| Syllable | Composition | Description |
|----------|-------------|-------------|
| 가 | ㄱ + ㅏ | No final, simple CV block |
| 강 | ㄱ + ㅏ + ㄱ | Final ㄱ placed underneath |
| 쾌 | ㅋ + ㅙ | Horizontal vowel, no final |
| 뱃 | ㅂ + ㅐ + ㅅ | Final ㅅ placed underneath |

## Historical Context & Theory
- **Hun min Jeong eum (훈민정음) proclamation (1446)** outlines the **four principles** of Hangul design:
  1. **상형** – shape reflects articulatory features.
  2. **관념** – abstract principles for vowel composition (sky‑earth‑human).
  3. **음성** – phonetic values are systematic and consistent.
  4. **이음** – letters combine to form syllables.
- **Modern Standard Korean** employs 19 initial consonants, 21 medial vowels, and 27 final consonant possibilities, yielding **11,172 possible syllable blocks** (19 × 21 × 28).
- **Unicode** – Hangul resides in three blocks: Hangul Compatibility Jamo (U+3130–U+318F), Hangul Jamo (U+1100–U+11FF), and Hangul Syllables (U+AC00–U+D7A3). A single code‑point can represent an entire syllable block.
- **Phonological theory** – Hangul’s featural design mirrors modern **feature geometry** used in generative phonology, making it an exemplary teaching tool for linguists.

---
## Practical Implications
- **Learning speed** – Studies show learners achieve basic literacy in ≈⅓ the time required for alphabetic scripts like English.
- **Computational use** – Because Hangul is a regular, algorithmic script, text processing (e.g., stemming, transliteration) can be performed by simple Unicode arithmetic: `syllable_code - 0xAC00` yields the indices of initial, medial, final.
- **Typography** – Modern fonts support dynamic composition of jamo, allowing custom rendering of historical or decorative Hangul forms.
